Designing the World Wide Web

Shortly after graduating college Mr. Berners-Lee started working as an engineer for a telecommunications company called Plessy. There, he would develop typesetting software for printers. This would set up his career becoming familiar with hypertext (a piece of text that is linked to another document that gives the user immediate access to specific information.)

His curiosity for the expansion of hypertext led to him creating a prototype system called "ENQUIRE." This program was similar to others (Apple's HyperCard, Memex, NLS, etc), but was able to run on many different systems and was portable. However, it lacked image rendering. ENQUIRE worked by linking various documents, or cards, together where users could simply click the link and open the "new page." Anyone with access to the program was able to add or remove cards. It worked mostly like a wiki then a series of web pages. It was a database for anyone who had access. Eventually this program would be fleshed out and the World Wide Web would be born.

Tim Berners-Lee came back to work at CERN in 1984 and used his ENQUIRE system daily. He found where the program was lacking. He needed to create a system where most of the time spent was keeping information up to date, but to allow anyone to update information. He wanted to create this same type of system, but accessible to everyone. He needed a program where users were able to link new cards without having to update old ones, or create new cards independent of others. He also wanted to add a feature where you could link to separate databases and a system able to handle a lot of user traffic. In 1989, CERN was considered the "largest internet node" in Europe. Berners-Lee saw this as an opportunity to combine his hyperlink text database with the Internet.

Here are two quotes from Tim Berners-Lee regarding the creation of the World Wide Web.

  • "I just had to take the hypertext idea and connect it to the TCP and DNS ideas and—ta-da!—the World Wide Web." — Tim Berners-Lee
  • "Creating the web was really an act of desperation, because the situation without it was very difficult when I was working at CERN later. Most of the technology involved in the web, like the hypertext, like the Internet, multifont text objects, had all been designed already. I just had to put them together. It was a step of generalizing, going to a higher level of abstraction, thinking about all the documentation systems out there as being possibly part of a larger imaginary documentation system." — Tim Berners-Lee

His efforts is the reason why you can click an image of a dog on google that takes you to a website that allows you to search for foster puppies in your area. It's the way we navigate the internet today. It's how this website has 4 clickable links at the top that take you to other web pages. Berners-Lee did all this as a way to help humanity. He created this as an open source program so as many people as possible could use it and create with it. His motivations were to help people share information, not for money or fame. In my opinion, this is the original roots of why the internet was so cool. With advertising agencies, social media companies, and large corporations, we're starting to lose the uniqueness of being able to share information freely and openly. Everything now-a-days has a paywall, or you need to enter personal information to create an account, everything you click is monitored and recorded and eventually used by some company to make money. Today, the internet feels more like a human farming tool than a free place to share information. This takes us further away from what the web was designed to do and the vision that Tim Berners-Lee had.
